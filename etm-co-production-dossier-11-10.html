Script Breakdown

Script #1

9:50 AM - 10:35 AM
Special Requirement: An additional person is needed to read the narration while filming to ensure timing is correct.

Talent Required:

• Clifton Williams

• Ms. Maggie's third grade class

• On-set narrator for timing

SCENE: Brightly lit, colorful classroom. Saturated colors. Clifton Williams in center frame, surrounded by instruments. Slow dolly zoom out. NARRATOR (warm, curious) This is Clifton Williams. CLIFTON (smiling) That's me. NARRATOR He's shared the stage with John Legend, directed Emmy-winning concerts, and performed for audiences around the world. NARRATOR (playful) So… what's he doing here — in a Denver public-school classroom? CLIFTON (grinning) Ask Education Through Music Colorado. Or better yet — ask them. STUDENTS (laughing, shouting) Can we play now?! (Music bursts in — percussion, laughter, rhythm. The classroom glows.) NARRATOR (soft, reflective) There's something magical happening here, especially when you realize that not long ago, this classroom was silent. (The camera slows — Clifton and students fade from frame. Empty chairs. Dusty instruments. Silence.) NARRATOR (somber) Like so many schools in Colorado, music education was lost to budget cuts — the instruments gathered dust, and the silence was heavy. NARRATOR (hopeful, inspiring) At a school near you, a teacher is still missing. When you help bring music back, you give children more than melodies — you give them confidence, belonging, and the courage to express who they are. (The setting returns to life — color, rhythm, and joy fill the room.) CO Gives Day Hook This Colorado Gives Day, donate to Education Through Music - Colorado and bring music back to our schools. Script #2

12:00 PM - 1:00 PM

Talent Required:

• Karissa (ETM-CO representative)

Location will be staged by ETM-CO ahead of time
SCENE: Direct-to-camera monologue — Karissa from Education Through Music–Colorado KARISSA (direct, warm) What do you remember about your elementary school music class? For many children in Colorado, the answer is nothing. Because they've never had one. KARISSA (earnest, conversational) Our schools are deeply underfunded, and music is often the first thing to disappear. Walk into some of these classrooms and you'll still see the traces of what once was — a dusty piano, silent instruments, the absence of a teacher who once filled the room with song. The music is gone. The classroom is quiet. (B-roll: joyful classroom → fades to dark, empty classroom.) KARISSA (warm, inspiring) At Education Through Music–Colorado, we believe music isn't an extra — it's essential. Research shows that when students make music, they build confidence, focus, and a love of learning that carries into everything they do. That's why we place dedicated, full-time music teachers in under-resourced public schools — making music part of every child's school day. We support our music teachers, and others in the state, in building engaging music programs for all learners. In these ways, we ensure students in marginalized communities have equitable access to the benefits of music education. (B-roll: unused instrument → student playing; empty desk → Clifton teaching; students smiling, moving, creating.) KARISSA (closing, heartfelt) Think back to your own music class — that teacher, that instrument, that song you played together that still lives in your mind. That mix of anticipation and pride standing on stage, your family watching. Help us give that memory to another child- because music changes everything. Script #3

1:00 PM - 2:30 PM Interviews

Talent Required:

• Parents (multiple for interviews)

• Students (multiple for interviews)

NARRATOR (soft, reflective) This music classroom is missing something. There's a piano, xylophones, recorders, desks… But where's the teacher? Where are the students? Where is the music? (B-roll: dim light, dust on a drum, a chair tipped forward. Silence.) NARRATOR (hopeful, rising) This classroom is the reality for so many students across Colorado in schools facing budget cuts. But at this school, something changed. B-Roll (Transition shot to showing active music class) STUDENT (smiling) We started having music class when I was in third grade, and it's my favorite class. PARENT (warm voice) I see it — she's more confident, more joyful every day. STUDENT (excited) Now I can show my family what I learned! PARENT (laughing softly) He sings to us after dinner — it fills the house again. STUDENT (beaming) When we play together, it feels like we can do anything. PARENT (emotional) Music gave my child a voice — and gave us something to share. (Montage: students performing, parents clapping, smiles, colors, rhythm.) NARRATOR (firm, inspiring) When school budgets are tight, music is the first program to be cut. And when music disappears, children lose more than songs — they lose confidence, curiosity, and the joy of being heard. But with your support, we can place dedicated, full-time music teachers in under-resourced public schools — making music part of every child's school day. Interview Questions

Questions for Parents:

  • How has music class impacted your child?
  • Does your child bring music home with them?
  • Was having a music class for your child important to you? Why?

Questions for Students:

  • What grade were you in when you had your first music class?
  • How do you feel about music class?
  • What are your favorite things about music class?
  • Who is your music teacher, what is he like?
  • What are your favorite things about Mr. Clifton?
